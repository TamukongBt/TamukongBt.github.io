### Why I Started Building Chakap

I've always found it strange how quickly important moments seem to disappear.

Not the moments themselves. Those stay with us in some invisible way. But the traces of them: the photos, the videos, the fragments people capture when something meaningful happens.

Think about a wedding, a graduation, or a big conference. Hundreds of people might be there, phones out, cameras flashing, videos rolling. Each person records their own version of the same moment: different angles, different emotions, different stories.

For a short while, those moments feel alive. Photos are shared in group chats, someone posts a highlight video, and a few images make it to social media.

Then slowly, everything scatters. I understand life must go on, but it always felt like the memory of that moment should be more than just a few photos on someone's phone or a forgotten folder in the cloud.

There might be pictures of you somewhere, smiling at a friend's wedding, that you never see. A video of your graduation that gets lost in a message thread. A photo from a conference that only the person next to you has.

Some pictures remain on a phone that gets replaced two years later. Others linger in a forgotten cloud folder or disappear into endless message threads. The event itself was shared, but the memories become fragmented.

***

### The Thought That Wouldn't Leave

At some point, I started asking a simple question:

If an event brings people together physically, why don't we have a place where the digital memories stay together too?

Right now, we store memories individually. My photos go into my gallery. Yours go into yours. The photographer sends a zip file somewhere. A few uploads reach social media.

But an event isn't an individual experience. It's a collective one. Dozens or hundreds of people share the same moment, each holding a small piece of the story.

What if those pieces could live together in one place?

***

### Not Just Another Photo App

When Chakap first began to take shape in my mind, I wasn't imagining another social platform. We already have plenty of those.

What interested me was something more intentional: the idea of events having their own digital home.

Not a random folder. Not a temporary chat. But a shared space devoted to the memory itself.

Imagine a wedding where every guest can add their photos to one common gallery. A graduation where students upload moments from the day. A conference where talks, photos, and videos sit together, all belonging to that specific event.

Instead of fragments scattered across the internet, the memory stays anchored to the moment that created it.

***

### The Shape of the Idea

The more I thought about it, the more it felt like events deserve their own kind of infrastructure.

An event could have:
- A shared media gallery.
- Live streaming for those who couldn't attend.
- Photobooth captures that appear instantly.
- Attendee uploads from their phones.
- Curated highlights created afterward.

And maybe, years later, someone revisits that event page and finds everything still there: not just the official footage, but everyone's perspective woven together into one story.

***

### Why It Matters to Me

Some of the most meaningful moments in life happen only once: a graduation, a wedding, a milestone celebration.

Those moments pass quickly, but the memories created around them can last decades. Still, we treat those memories almost casually, scattering them across platforms built for everything else.

I kept wondering: what if we built something designed specifically to preserve those shared experiences?

That curiosity eventually became Chakap.

***

### Where It Might Go

Chakap is still evolving. Like any early idea, it's being shaped slowly piece by piece, feature by feature. But the vision behind it remains clear.

Events should not vanish when they end. They should leave behind a place where the memory of that moment still lives, not as scattered fragments, but as a shared story.

That is the idea I'm trying to build.
