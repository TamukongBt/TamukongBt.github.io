b:T10cd,<h3>Why I Started Building Chakap</h3>
<p>I've always found it strange how quickly important moments seem to disappear.</p>
<p>Not the moments themselves. Those stay with us in some invisible way. But the traces of them: the photos, the videos, the fragments people capture when something meaningful happens.</p>
<p>Think about a wedding, a graduation, or a big conference. Hundreds of people might be there, phones out, cameras flashing, videos rolling. Each person records their own version of the same moment: different angles, different emotions, different stories.</p>
<p>For a short while, those moments feel alive. Photos are shared in group chats, someone posts a highlight video, and a few images make it to social media.</p>
<p>Then slowly, everything scatters. I understand life must go on, but it always felt like the memory of that moment should be more than just a few photos on someone's phone or a forgotten folder in the cloud.</p>
<p>There might be pictures of you somewhere, smiling at a friend's wedding, that you never see. A video of your graduation that gets lost in a message thread. A photo from a conference that only the person next to you has.</p>
<p>Some pictures remain on a phone that gets replaced two years later. Others linger in a forgotten cloud folder or disappear into endless message threads. The event itself was shared, but the memories become fragmented.</p>
<hr>
<h3>The Thought That Wouldn't Leave</h3>
<p>At some point, I started asking a simple question:</p>
<p>If an event brings people together physically, why don't we have a place where the digital memories stay together too?</p>
<p>Right now, we store memories individually. My photos go into my gallery. Yours go into yours. The photographer sends a zip file somewhere. A few uploads reach social media.</p>
<p>But an event isn't an individual experience. It's a collective one. Dozens or hundreds of people share the same moment, each holding a small piece of the story.</p>
<p>What if those pieces could live together in one place?</p>
<hr>
<h3>Not Just Another Photo App</h3>
<p>When Chakap first began to take shape in my mind, I wasn't imagining another social platform. We already have plenty of those.</p>
<p>What interested me was something more intentional: the idea of events having their own digital home.</p>
<p>Not a random folder. Not a temporary chat. But a shared space devoted to the memory itself.</p>
<p>Imagine a wedding where every guest can add their photos to one common gallery. A graduation where students upload moments from the day. A conference where talks, photos, and videos sit together, all belonging to that specific event.</p>
<p>Instead of fragments scattered across the internet, the memory stays anchored to the moment that created it.</p>
<hr>
<h3>The Shape of the Idea</h3>
<p>The more I thought about it, the more it felt like events deserve their own kind of infrastructure.</p>
<p>An event could have:</p>
<ul>
<li>A shared media gallery.</li>
<li>Live streaming for those who couldn't attend.</li>
<li>Photobooth captures that appear instantly.</li>
<li>Attendee uploads from their phones.</li>
<li>Curated highlights created afterward.</li>
</ul>
<p>And maybe, years later, someone revisits that event page and finds everything still there: not just the official footage, but everyone's perspective woven together into one story.</p>
<hr>
<h3>Why It Matters to Me</h3>
<p>Some of the most meaningful moments in life happen only once: a graduation, a wedding, a milestone celebration.</p>
<p>Those moments pass quickly, but the memories created around them can last decades. Still, we treat those memories almost casually, scattering them across platforms built for everything else.</p>
<p>I kept wondering: what if we built something designed specifically to preserve those shared experiences?</p>
<p>That curiosity eventually became Chakap.</p>
<hr>
<h3>Where It Might Go</h3>
<p>Chakap is still evolving. Like any early idea, it's being shaped slowly piece by piece, feature by feature. But the vision behind it remains clear.</p>
<p>Events should not vanish when they end. They should leave behind a place where the memory of that moment still lives, not as scattered fragments, but as a shared story.</p>
<p>That is the idea I'm trying to build.</p>
